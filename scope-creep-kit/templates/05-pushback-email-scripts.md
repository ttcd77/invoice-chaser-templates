# Pushback Email Scripts — 5 Professional Ways to Say "That's Out of Scope"

> Saying no to scope creep is uncomfortable. These scripts give you the words when you don't have them. Customize the tone for your relationship with each client, but keep the structure — acknowledge, explain, offer a path forward.

---

## Script 1: The Gentle Redirect

**Use when:** A client casually asks for "one small thing" that's actually new scope. They're not being difficult — they just don't realize it's outside the agreement.

**Subject:** Quick note on the [feature/change] you mentioned

Hi [Name],

Thanks for bringing this up. I took a look at what it would take to [add the feature / make the change].

This is actually outside what we scoped in our original agreement ([link/reference the SOW section]). It's not huge — probably about [X hours / $X] in additional work — but I want to make sure we're on the same page before I dive in.

If you'd like me to proceed, I'll send over a quick change order form with the updated timeline and cost. No rush — just want to be transparent.

Best,
[Your name]

**Why this works:** It's friendly, assumes good faith, and presents the change order as a formality rather than a confrontation. The client feels informed, not rejected.

---

## Script 2: The Three-Option Framework

**Use when:** A client asks for something significant that you're happy to do, but it changes the project scope meaningfully. Give them choices instead of a simple yes/no.

**Subject:** Options for the [feature/change] you requested

Hi [Name],

I've reviewed what you're asking for and I have three paths we can take:

**Option A — Do it as a Change Order:** I add this to the project as a separate work item. Estimated additional cost: [$X]. Timeline extension: [X days].

**Option B — Swap it in:** We replace [existing deliverable] with this new request. No cost change, but we lose [what gets replaced].

**Option C — Save it for Phase 2:** We finish the current scope first (keeping us on schedule and on budget), then tackle this as a follow-up project after launch.

I'm happy with whichever path works best for you. Option C is what I usually recommend — get the core project shipped, then layer on extras once we see how it performs.

Let me know what you think.

Best,
[Your name]

**Why this works:** You're not saying no — you're giving the client agency. And Option C ("save it for later") is often the most appealing because it doesn't derail the current timeline. Psychologically, you've reframed "no" as "not now."

## Script 3: The Scope Reminder

**Use when:** A client keeps adding "small requests" that are piling up. You need to reset expectations without damaging the relationship.

**Subject:** Checking in on project scope

Hi [Name],

I want to make sure we're tracking well toward our deadline. Over the past [week / two weeks], we've added a few items that fall outside our original scope:

- [Item 1 — brief description]
- [Item 2 — brief description]
- [Item 3 — brief description]

Individually these are small, but together they add about [X hours] of additional work. I'm happy to include them — I just want to be transparent that they'll affect both the timeline and the total.

I've put together a quick summary of what was in the original agreement vs. what's been added since. Want me to send it over so we can decide which items to prioritize?

Best,
[Your name]

**Why this works:** You're not blaming the client for scope creep — you're framing it as a collaborative check-in. Listing the items makes the creep visible, and offering to prioritize gives the client control while protecting your time.

---

## Script 4: The Firm Boundary

**Use when:** A client is repeatedly pushing scope and you need to draw a clear line. This is for clients who've ignored gentler nudges.

**Subject:** Scope clarification before we continue

Hi [Name],

I've enjoyed working on this project, but I need to flag something so we can get back on track.

Our original agreement covered [summary of scope]. Since we started, additional requests have been added that fall outside that scope, including [brief list]. I've accommodated several of these at no additional charge, but at this point, continuing without a formal scope adjustment doesn't work for either of us — it pushes deadlines, dilutes the quality of the core deliverables, and creates ambiguity about what's included.

Going forward, here's how I'd like to handle new requests:

- Any work outside the original SOW requires a signed change order before I begin
- Change orders include the cost and timeline impact upfront
- I won't begin out-of-scope work without this in place

I'm still 100% committed to delivering a great result on the core project. This just ensures we both have clarity on what that includes.

Happy to hop on a quick call if you'd rather talk through it.

Best,
[Your name]

**Why this works:** It's direct without being hostile. You acknowledge the work you've already done for free (building goodwill), then set a clear boundary with a defined process. The offer to call humanizes it.

## Script 5: The "No" That Leads to Yes

**Use when:** The client wants something you genuinely can't or shouldn't do — wrong fit, wrong timing, or a request you know will fail. Saying no the right way can actually strengthen trust.

**Subject:** My honest take on [the request]

Hi [Name],

I've thought about this and I want to give you my honest take — I don't think [the request] is the right move right now, and here's why:

[1-2 sentences explaining why — wrong timing, won't solve the actual problem, conflicts with the project goals, etc.]

Instead, here's what I'd suggest: [alternative approach that actually serves the client's underlying need].

The reason I'm pushing back is that I want this project to succeed for you, and I think [the request] would [dilute / delay / complicate] what we're already building.

If you still want to pursue it, I'm happy to figure out a path. Just wanted to give you my unvarnished opinion first.

Best,
[Your name]

**Why this works:** Clients hire you for expertise, not just execution. Saying no thoughtfully — with a better alternative — demonstrates that expertise. It builds trust because the client knows you'll tell them the truth, not just take their money.

## Quick Reference: Which Script to Use

| Situation | Script |
|-----------|--------|
| Client casually asks for "one small thing" | #1 Gentle Redirect |
| Client wants something substantial, you're open to it | #2 Three-Option Framework |
| Multiple small requests are adding up | #3 Scope Reminder |
| Client keeps pushing after gentler nudges | #4 Firm Boundary |
| Client wants something you genuinely think is a bad idea | #5 No That Leads to Yes |

## General Principles for Scope Conversations

1. **Don't respond immediately.** When a client asks for something out of scope, say "Let me look at what that involves and get back to you." This gives you time to think and prevents knee-jerk yeses.

2. **Always present the cost.** Every scope conversation should include time, money, or both. If the client doesn't know the cost, they have no reason to stop asking.

3. **Document everything.** Verbal agreements are scope creep's best friend. After any scope conversation, send a quick email: "Just to confirm, we agreed to [change] for an additional [$X], extending the deadline to [date]."

4. **The first "yes" to scope creep is the hardest to undo.** It's much easier to set boundaries on project day 1 than project day 100.
